# 300mm Wafer Fab Economics

> [!info] Summary
> The shift from 200mm to 300mm wafers transformed semiconductor manufacturing economics by increasing die output per process run and improving cost efficiency, but it also raised the capital intensity and scale threshold required to compete. In modern semiconductors, 300mm fabs are both an economic advantage and a strategic barrier to entry.

## Definition

A 300mm wafer fab manufactures chips on 12-inch silicon wafers rather than older 200mm 8-inch wafers. Because wafer area scales with the square of diameter, a 300mm wafer provides materially more usable silicon area per processed wafer, enabling lower cost per die when yields and utilization are healthy. Nearly all advanced logic and memory fabs today use 300mm wafers.

The economic significance goes beyond simple geometry. Moving to 300mm also required more automation, larger tools, cleaner transport systems, and different factory layouts. That means the 300mm transition was both a unit-cost improvement and an industry-consolidation event.

## Context and origin

The semiconductor industry historically progressed from smaller wafers to larger ones to lower cost per transistor. The 200mm era dominated many analog, MCU, and mature-node categories, while the 300mm era became the standard for high-volume logic and memory. Companies that built strong 300mm manufacturing networks gained structural cost advantages, especially in high-volume segments.

For foundries and IDMs, the move to 300mm also widened the gulf between leaders and followers. Building a modern 300mm fab required enormous capex, automation expertise, and supplier coordination. As a result, fewer companies could afford to participate, and scale itself became a moat.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The basic economic case is wafer output. A 300mm wafer has roughly 2.25 times the surface area of a 200mm wafer, although usable die gain depends on edge losses and die size. That means more die per run, better labor efficiency, and often lower utility and overhead cost per die when the fab is well utilized.

Tool economics matter just as much. 300mm fabs were designed for greater automation, including FOUP-based wafer handling and highly integrated production control. This reduces contamination risk and labor intensity. But it also means the upfront capital stack is larger, and the depreciation burden is far heavier. A new fab that runs below target utilization becomes financially painful quickly.

The economics vary by product. For high-volume logic, memory, and some analog lines, 300mm is an obvious advantage. But not every specialty process or legacy product migrates easily. Some embedded, analog, MEMS, and mature-node products remain on 200mm because redesigning processes or qualifying new sites is too costly relative to the benefit. That is why the industry still faces 200mm bottlenecks even while celebrating 300mm scale.

At the leading edge, 300mm economics are now intertwined with [[EUV Lithography Market Analysis]]. The wafer is only one part of the cost stack; the far larger story is the immense depreciation burden from EUV scanners, metrology, and advanced process control. This makes capacity planning and utilization central to foundry profitability.

## Nuances critiques limits

A key nuance is that larger wafers do not automatically mean lower cost per good die. Yield, cycle time, and product mix matter enormously. A poorly ramped 300mm fab with weak yield can be less economical than a mature, stable 200mm line for certain products.

Another issue is strategic rigidity. Once a company commits tens of billions to a 300mm campus, it has to keep loading those fabs. That can distort pricing behavior, capital allocation, and customer negotiations. Scale is a moat, but it can also become a trap if demand assumptions prove wrong.

There is also a policy angle. Governments often subsidize 300mm fabs for strategic reasons, but public discussion can understate the long period required to reach efficient utilization and acceptable returns. New fab announcements generate headlines immediately; economic success does not.
