# DARPA Electronics Resurgence

> [!info] Summary
> DARPA's Electronics Resurgence Initiative (ERI) is a multi-year research program launched in 2017 to reinvest in US semiconductor research following growing concern about declining domestic semiconductor innovation relative to global competitors, funding programs in novel materials, chip architectures, design tools, and security through university and industry performers.

## Definition

DARPA ERI was launched in response to the recognition that the United States, while leading in semiconductor design and manufacturing equipment, had allowed fundamental semiconductor research capacity to atrophy relative to the 1980s-1990s era. The program funds research across: materials and integration (novel semiconductor materials, GaN, SiC, photonics), architectures (specialized processors, 3D stacking, in-memory computing), design (automated design tools, security-by-design), and supply chain security.

ERI funding was approximately $1.5B over 5 years (2018-2022), with additional follow-on programs continuing. The program has been credited with jumpstarting new investment in domestic semiconductor research and was a precursor to the CHIPS Act.

## Context and origin

ERI emerged from the 2015-2016 period when DARPA and the DoD recognized that US semiconductor capability had become over-reliant on offshore manufacturing, and that the research pipeline that had driven prior US semiconductor leadership was underfunded. The 2017 launch coincided with growing public awareness of semiconductor supply chain vulnerabilities.

Key programs under ERI include: DAHI (Diversified Access to Heterogeneous Integration), 3DS (3D Solar), CHIPS (Common Heterogeneous Integration and Intellectual Property Reuse), FRAME (Foundations for Array Modeling and Electrical co-design), and SOS (Security with 0 Trust in Microelectronics).

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The program's structure funds performers (universities, research institutions, companies) to develop specific capabilities that have defense applications but also broader commercial potential. This dual-use approach is intentional, maximizing the return on defense research investment by allowing commercial adoption.

CHIPS (Common Heterogeneous Integration) specifically addressed the chiplet and advanced packaging challenge, funding the development of open chiplet standards and interconnects that would allow defense systems to use commercial chiplets with heterogeneous integration while maintaining security and reliability. The link to [[Advanced Packaging Technology Overview]] and [[Chiplet Based Design Ecosystem]] is direct.

[[Wide Bandgap Material Economics]] research was funded under ERI's Materials and Integration thrust, supporting GaN and SiC research for defense radar and power applications.

## Nuances critiques limits

The main criticism of DARPA ERI is the challenge of transitioning research results to production. Many DARPA programs produce impressive research results that never reach commercial or defense production due to the gap between research demonstration and manufacturing readiness. The Trusted Foundry Program was partially an attempt to address this gap.

The CHIPS Act (2022) represents a larger-scale attempt to address the same underlying concerns that motivated ERI, but with direct manufacturing subsidies rather than research funding alone.
