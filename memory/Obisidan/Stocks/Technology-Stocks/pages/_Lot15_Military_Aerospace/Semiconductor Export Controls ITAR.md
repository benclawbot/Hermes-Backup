# Semiconductor Export Controls ITAR

> [!info] Summary
> US export controls on semiconductors classified under ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) restrict the sale and transfer of defense semiconductor technology, creating compliance burdens for semiconductor companies serving both defense and commercial markets and driving some international customers to seek ITAR-free alternatives.

## Definition

ITAR controls cover defense articles on the US Munitions List (USML), which includes certain advanced semiconductors with defense applications: radiation-hardened ICs, secure processors, GaN power amplifiers for radar, advanced A/D and D/A converters for EW, and certain types of RF semiconductor components. Export of ITAR-controlled items requires a license from the State Department.

The related EAR (Export Administration Regulations) administered by the Commerce Department controls dual-use semiconductors that have both military and commercial applications, including many compound semiconductor materials, certain fabrication equipment, and advanced integrated circuits.

## Context and origin

ITAR originated from the Arms Export Control Act of 1976 and is administered by the US State Department's Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC). The regulations are designed to control the export of defense articles and services to protect national security.

The strategic concern driving ITAR semiconductor controls is preventing adversary access to semiconductor technology that would improve their weapons systems. This includes preventing China, Russia, and other adversaries from acquiring advanced RF components (for radar and communications jammers), secure processors, or radiation-hardened components (for space systems).

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

Compliance requirements for semiconductor companies include: ITAR registration with DDTC, technology control plans (TCPs) to segregate ITAR-controlled information, export license applications for controlled semiconductor exports, and deemed export license requirements for foreign nationals working on ITAR-controlled technology in the US.

The deemed export rule is particularly significant: a foreign national in the US working on ITAR-controlled semiconductor technology may require an export license, even though no actual export is occurring. This complicates talent management at semiconductor companies.

The [[Trusted Foundry Program]] provides a framework for controlling defense semiconductor manufacturing. [[Defense Semiconductor Requirements]] often overlap with ITAR requirements for specific defense applications.

## Nuances critiques limits

The primary criticism of ITAR is that it puts US semiconductor companies at a competitive disadvantage in commercial markets because allied nations may refuse to buy US-origin semiconductors that carry ITAR restrictions (creating support and documentation burdens). This has driven some allied countries to develop ITAR-free alternatives using non-US semiconductor suppliers.

The distinction between ITAR-controlled and dual-use (EAR-controlled) semiconductor technology is not always clear, creating compliance complexity for semiconductor companies.

The 2022-2024 semiconductor export control packages targeting China represent a significant expansion of semiconductor-specific controls beyond traditional ITAR, extending EAR controls to advanced logic, memory, and semiconductor manufacturing equipment.
