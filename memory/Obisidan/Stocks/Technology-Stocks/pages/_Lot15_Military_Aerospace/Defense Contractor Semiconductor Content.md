# Defense Contractor Semiconductor Content

> [!info] Summary
> Major defense contractors — Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, Boeing, General Dynamics, L3Harris — are significant buyers of specialized semiconductor components for military platforms, but their semiconductor supply chain is largely dependent on the specialized supplier base covered by [[Defense Semiconductor Requirements]] and [[Trusted Foundry Program]].

## Definition

Defense contractors integrate semiconductor components into platforms: fighter aircraft (F-35, F/A-18, F-15), missiles (JASSM, LRASM, Tomahawk), ships (Aegis, DDG-1000), ground vehicles (Abrams, Bradley, AMPV), and space systems (SBIRS, WGS, GPS III). Each platform type has different semiconductor content and supply chain characteristics.

The semiconductor content per platform varies enormously. A modern fighter jet like F-35 contains thousands of specialized semiconductor components worth hundreds of millions of dollars per aircraft. A missile contains fewer but highly specialized components (radar seekers, guidance processors, secure communications).

## Context and origin

The defense industry consolidated significantly from the 1990s to the 2010s, reducing the number of prime contractors capable of building major defense systems. Lockheed Martin (formed from Loral and Martin Marietta mergers), Raytheon (multiple mergers), Northrop Grumman (TRW, Orbital ATK mergers), and Boeing (McDonnell Douglas merger) dominate the prime contractor landscape.

This consolidation shifted bargaining power somewhat toward primes in their relationships with subsystem suppliers, but the specialized nature of defense semiconductor requirements limits the ability of primes to easily switch semiconductor suppliers.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

RaytheonMissiles & Defense builds radar (AN/APG-79, AN/SPY-6), missiles (AIM-120 AMRAAM, SM-6), and sensor systems with high semiconductor content. Northrop Grumman's Aerospace sector builds radar (AN/APG-77, AN/APG-81 for F-35), space systems, and无人机. Lockheed Martin's Missiles and Fire Control builds missiles and fire control systems; Aeronautics builds F-35 and other aircraft.

Semiconductor content in these systems is dominated by: AESA radar T/R modules (GaN PAs), secure processors, radiation-hardened components for space, high-speed data converters for EW and radar, and specialized power management for missile and aviation environments.

## Nuances critiques limits

Defense contractors face semiconductor supply chain risk because qualified components have long design cycles and the supplier base is concentrated. Programs spanning 20-40 years may face component obsolescence, requiring requalification of alternative sources.

The major contractors are increasingly vertically integrating into semiconductor capabilities. Boeing's acquisition of semiconductor facilities, Raytheon's internal component manufacturing, and Northrop Grumman's internal ASIC development are all attempts to control critical technology supply.

The CHIPS Act subsidies for defense semiconductor manufacturing benefit contractors who are building domestic semiconductor capacity to support national security programs.
