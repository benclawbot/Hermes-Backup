# Trusted Foundry Program

> [!info] Summary
> The Trusted Foundry Program establishes a secure domestic supply chain for defense semiconductor components, certifying commercial foundries and assembly facilities to handle classified or sensitive defense electronics. It addresses concerns about malicious modifications, counterfeits, and foreign ownership of semiconductor manufacturing capacity serving US defense systems.

## Definition

The Trusted Foundry Program (TFP) certifies semiconductor manufacturing facilities to produce controlled defense electronics. Certification requires: secure facility access controls, controlled access to design and manufacturing data, personnel security screening, automated inspection and traceability systems, and compliance with [[Defense Semiconductor Requirements]] specifications.

Certified facilities include: certain TSMC fabs (for commercial-leading edge nodes under special agreements), Samsung Foundry (for certain defense-related work), GlobalFoundries (for radiation-tolerant and specialty processes), and internal defense fabs at companies like BAE Systems and Northrop Grumman.

## Context and origin

The TFP was established in the early 2000s following concerns about counterfeit components entering the defense supply chain and the recognition that much of the US defense electronics manufacturing had migrated offshore. The program was administered by the Defense Microelectronics Activity (DMEA).

The program's scope has evolved with the semiconductor industry. Initially focused on older (submicron) nodes for which domestic capacity existed, the program has expanded to include leading-edge nodes through special agreements with TSMC and Samsung, addressing the reality that modern defense systems increasingly require advanced semiconductor processes.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The certification process involves: facility audits by DMEA, security plan development and review, facility protection assessments, and ongoing surveillance. Certified fabs operate under "trusted wafer lot acceptance criteria" that exceed standard commercial quality requirements.

The program's relationship with TSMC is notable. While TSMC is a foreign company, its Arizona fab (TSMC Arizona, N4 and N3 processes) operates under TFP certification, providing leading-edge domestic capacity for defense. The CHIPS Act subsidies for TSMC Arizona were partly motivated by the strategic desire to have leading-edge foundry capacity within trusted access.

## Nuances critiques limits

The program faces ongoing tension between the need for leading-edge processes (only available offshore or at TSMC Arizona) and the desire to maintain trusted domestic capability. The fact that most leading-edge manufacturing remains outside trusted facilities is a structural vulnerability.

The emergence of Chiplet and [[Advanced Packaging Technology Overview]] approaches adds complexity to trusted supply chain requirements, because packaging and assembly operations may also need trusted status.

The growth of Chinese semiconductor manufacturing (and the concern about Chinese government access to semiconductor supply chains) has renewed focus on trusted sourcing, particularly for [[Radiation Hardened Chips Space]] where space systems may be particularly vulnerable to supply chain compromise.
