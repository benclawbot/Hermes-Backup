# OLED Display Technology

> [!info] Summary
> OLED (organic light-emitting diode) displays emit light directly from each pixel, enabling perfect blacks, infinite contrast ratios, thinner devices, and faster response times compared to LCD. OLED has become the dominant premium smartphone display technology and is expanding into laptops, monitors, tablets, and eventually televisions, with Samsung Display as the primary manufacturing leader and LG Display competing in large-area TVs.

## Definition

OLED displays use thin films of organic compounds that emit light when electrical current is applied. Unlike LCD, which requires a backlight, each OLED pixel generates its own light. This allows individual pixels to be turned completely off, producing true blacks and dramatically improved contrast. The technology supports flexible substrates, enabling curved and foldable displays.

OLED manufacturing involves vacuum deposition of organic materials onto TFT (thin-film transistor) backplanes through a shadow mask (fine metal mask, FMM) in a process called evaporation. The backplane is typically LTPS (low-temperature polysilicon) or oxide TFT on glass or flexible polyimide substrates.

## Context and origin

OLED technology was first commercialized by Sony in 2004 for small cameras and portable devices, but Samsung Display is credited with solving the manufacturing challenges that made OLED viable for smartphones starting with the Galaxy S series in 2010. The company invested billions in OLED fabs during years of losses before achieving manufacturing yields that made the technology profitable.

LG Display commercialized large-area OLED TVs starting in 2013, using WRGB (white + RGB color filter) architecture. The TV OLED business has remained less profitable than Samsung's small-to-medium OLED business due to lower yields and higher manufacturing costs for large-area substrates.

Apple's adoption of OLED for iPhone X in 2017 validated OLED as the premium smartphone display technology and created enormous demand for Samsung Display panels, making Apple one of Samsung's largest display customers.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

OLED manufacturing complexity is extraordinary. The organic materials are extremely sensitive to moisture and oxygen, requiring hermetic encapsulation. The evaporation process requires precise alignment of multiple organic layers through FMM shadow masks. Any particle contamination or mask misalignment causes dead pixels or color variation.

Samsung Display's competitive advantage comes from years of manufacturing learning that has improved yields faster than competitors. The company's Gen 6 and Gen 8.7 OLED fabs are optimized for smartphone and IT panel production respectively, with proprietary material formulations and process knowledge that have not been fully replicated.

The IT OLED expansion — OLED panels for laptops and monitors — is the next battleground. Samsung Display is building Gen 8.7 fabs optimized for IT glass sizes. BOE and other Chinese manufacturers are also investing heavily in IT OLED. This transition is analyzed in [[Display Panel Industry Overview]].

Foldable OLED represents the frontier of smartphone display innovation. Samsung Display's UTG (ultra-thin glass) and Huawei's CPI (colorless polyimide) represent two material approaches to protecting flexible OLED displays. Each approach involves tradeoffs between optical clarity, durability, and manufacturing cost.

## Nuances critiques limits

OLED's main weakness is lifespan and burn-in. Organic materials degrade over time, with blue pixels degrading faster than red or green, causing color shift. Burn-in (permanent image retention from static content) remains a concern for TV and monitor use cases. Manufacturers have developed pixel-shift algorithms, panel rotation, and improved materials to mitigate these issues.

Manufacturing yield remains the key determinant of profitability. Samsung Display's yield advantage over BOE and other competitors translates directly into cost and quality advantages that competitors struggle to close even with similar equipment and capital investment.

The material cost structure is also important. OLED organic materials (emitting dopants, hosts, ETL, HIL) are expensive and require specialized chemical suppliers. This gives materials vendors like Idemitsu Kosan, Merck, and Universal Display Corporation significant leverage.
