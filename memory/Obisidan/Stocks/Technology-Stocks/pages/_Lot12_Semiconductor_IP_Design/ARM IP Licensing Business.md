# ARM IP Licensing Business

> [!info] Summary
> Arm Holdings is the foundational intellectual property company of the semiconductor industry, licensing the processor architecture used in virtually all smartphones, most data center CPUs, and a growing share of IoT and edge devices. Its royalty-based model generates high-margin recurring revenue from essentially every major mobile and server chip, giving it a structural position analogous to a toll road for modern computing.

## Definition

Arm Holdings licenses processor architectures, CPU core designs, GPU designs, interconnect IP, and system IP to chipmakers worldwide. Customers include Apple, Qualcomm, Samsung, MediaTek, NVIDIA, AMD, Amazon, and hundreds of smaller companies. Arm does not manufacture chips; it designs the blueprints and licenses the right to implement them.

The company operates in two segments: licensing (one-time fees and configuration charges) and royalties (per-chip revenue based on the type and selling price of the chip). This hybrid model means Arm benefits from both new chip program starts and the volume of chips shipped.

## Context and origin

Arm (originally Acorn Computers) was founded in 1990 as a joint venture between Acorn, Apple, and VLSI Technology. The RISC-based architecture was designed for power efficiency rather than peak performance, making it ideal for mobile devices. Apple's iPhone launch in 2007 cemented Arm as the dominant mobile architecture, and the smartphone boom created the royalty volume that made Arm highly profitable.

SoftBank acquired Arm in 2016 for $32 billion. Nvidia attempted to acquire Arm from SoftBank in 2020 for $40 billion but abandoned the deal in 2022 after regulatory opposition from US, UK, and EU authorities. Arm then went public in September 2023, raising $9.7 billion in one of the largest tech IPOs of the decade.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

Arm's licensing tiers offer different levels of customization. Architecture licenses let customers design their own Arm-compatible cores (Apple does this). PU (Physical IP) licenses let customers implement Arm's processor designs. Total Access licenses bundle multiple IP blocks at negotiated prices. The flexibility lets Arm serve both customers wanting quick-to-market standard implementations and those wanting deep customization.

Royalty rates vary by chip type and market. High-end smartphone application processors generate higher per-chip royalties than low-end IoT chips because royalty is typically a percentage of chip ASP. This means Arm benefits disproportionately from premium devices, giving it an unusual asymmetry where ASP trends in the overall chip market affect its revenue.

The competitive position is detailed in [[Semiconductor IP Licensing]]. ARM's dominance in mobile CPU architecture is near-absolute because Qualcomm, Samsung, MediaTek, and Apple all build on ARM. Even Huawei's HiSilicon designed Kirin chips on ARM until US export restrictions interrupted the relationship.

The threat from [[RISC-V Architecture]] is growing but limited. RISC-V is an open-source architecture gaining traction in IoT, microcontrollers, and some storage applications. However, displacing ARM in premium mobile and server applications would require rewriting enormous amounts of software, making the threat to ARM's core markets slower-moving than often described.

## Nuances critiques limits

Arm's per-chip royalty exposure means it is structurally dependent on smartphone unit volumes, which are cyclical. A prolonged smartphone downturn directly compresses ARM's royalty revenue even if its licensing pipeline remains strong.

Another risk is customer vertical integration. Apple, Amazon (Graviton), and other large customers have the scale to develop or license alternatives. Apple has already replaced some ARM licenses with internally developed cores for certain applications, reducing ARM's per-chip take. As custom silicon proliferates (see [[Apple Silicon Competition]]), this structural headwind grows.

Finally, post-IPO governance and strategic direction remain somewhat uncertain. SoftBank's ownership was decisive but also constrained investment horizons. The IPO introduced new accountability to public shareholders while also creating questions about whether ARM will prioritize long-term platform investment over near-term profitability.
