# Test Equipment Market Teradyne

> [!info] Summary
> The semiconductor test equipment market is dominated by Teradyne (via its LitePoint subsidiary for wireless test) and Advantest, with Teradyne holding the leading position in SoC test systems and Advantest dominant in memory test. Test equipment (ATE — automatic test equipment) is essential for verifying chip functionality before packaging and after assembly, with new requirements for chiplet testing and advanced packaging creating product development opportunities.

## Definition

ATE (automatic test equipment) systems test semiconductor chips during and after manufacturing. Test types include: wafer sort (testing individual chips on the wafer before dicing), final test (testingpackaged parts), burn-in (accelerated life testing to eliminate early failures), RF test (for wireless chips), and system-level test (testing the chip in a real system environment).

Key ATE parameters: pin count (number of signals that can be tested simultaneously), timing accuracy, test throughput (chips per hour), and the ability to test advanced features (high-speed SerDes, DDR5, PCIe, HBM).

## Context and origin

The ATE industry consolidated significantly from the 1990s to the 2010s. Teradyne acquired GenRad (1995), Mega Chips (2000s), LitePoint (2011), and others. Advantest acquired Verigy (2011). The result is a duopoly in SoC test: Teradyne and Advantest dominate, with COIN (Cohu) as a distant third.

Teradyne's acquisition of LitePoint in 2011 gave it leadership in wireless test (WiFi, Bluetooth, cellular), while Advantest has traditionally dominated memory test (DRAM and NAND flash testing equipment).

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

Teradyne's FLEX and UltraFlex test systems are widely used for SoC testing across applications from mobile to data center. The company's J750 is a cost-effective solution for high-volume mobile chip testing. The IGXL is used for wireless chip test.

The link to [[Chiplet Based Design Ecosystem]] creates a new testing challenge: each chiplet in a multi-chip package must be tested individually before assembly (KGD testing), and the completed package must be tested at system-level. This drives demand for new test approaches and potentially new equipment categories.

[[Fan Out Wafer Level Packaging]] and [[Advanced Packaging Technology Overview]] create similar test challenges: stacked die, interposer-based packages, and embedded components require more sophisticated test capabilities.

## Nuances critiques limits

The ATE market is highly cyclical, tracking semiconductor unit volumes. The 2022-2023 semiconductor downturn reduced test equipment demand significantly. The recovery that began in late 2023 was driven by AI chip production, which requires very sophisticated and expensive test equipment.

The test equipment industry faces a long-term challenge: as chips become more integrated (SoC, SiP, chiplets), the amount of test content per chip may decline, reducing the market opportunity even as chip volumes grow. However, the complexity of new interfaces (HBM, PCIe 5.0, 112G SerDes) drives higher test equipment cost that partially offsets volume/unit price trends.
