# 2.5D Interposer Technologies

> [!info] Summary
> 2.5D interposer packaging uses a passive silicon or organic interposer placed between chips and the package substrate to achieve very high-density chip-to-chip interconnections at lower power than standard flip-chip. Intel's EMIB and TSMC's CoWoS are the dominant commercial approaches, enabling high-bandwidth memory (HBM) integration with AI accelerators and CPUs.

## Definition

A 2.5D interposer is a silicon or organic substrate with fine-pitch metal routing layers that sits between the chip(s) and the organic package substrate. Chips are mounted directly on the interposer (flip-chip), and the interposer routes connections between chips and to the substrate. The key advantage is the ability to connect chips at very short distances with very high I/O density, enabling HBM memory integration and multi-chip communication without the cost of full 3D stacking.

Silicon interposers use TSV-like processes (but only a few TSVs rather than dense vertical stacking) to connect the top surface to the bottom substrate. Organic interposers (like Intel's EMIB) use embedded bridge dies in an organic substrate.

## Context and origin

2.5D interposer packaging originated in the 2000s for FPGA and graphics applications. Xilinx (now AMD) used 2.5D interposers for their Virtex-7 family to achieve high-bandwidth memory integration before HBM was even introduced. The Virtex-7 2000T was the first high-volume 2.5D product.

TSMC's CoWoS (Chip on Wafer on Substrate) became the dominant approach for AI accelerators starting with NVIDIA's Pascal architecture (2016) and continuing through Volta, Ampere, and Hopper GPU generations. AMD's Zen architecture uses interposer-based packaging for connecting CPU cores to I/O and memory controllers.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

TSMC CoWoS uses a silicon interposer with high-density routing layers. The interposer is made in TSMC's frontend fabs using processes similar to standard wafer fabrication, which is why only TSMC and Samsung (with their own foundry capabilities) can offer CoWoS. The interposer is attached to a standard organic substrate.

Intel EMIB (Embedded Multi-Diet Interconnect Bridge) uses a small silicon bridge die embedded in the organic package substrate. The bridge provides high-density interconnects only where needed (e.g., connecting the CPU to HBM), while the rest of the package uses standard organic routing. EMIB is lower cost than full silicon interposer because only a small bridge die is used.

The [[Chiplet Based Design Ecosystem]] connects to 2.5D interposers as the primary commercial application. AMD's [[Chiplet Based Design Ecosystem]] uses interposers for their data center CPUs.

## Nuances critiques limits

The cost of silicon interposer packaging is very high — the interposer itself can add $50-100+ to the chip package cost. This limits 2.5D interposer use to high-value products like AI accelerators, GPUs, and high-end CPUs. Mobile applications cannot justify this cost.

The organic interposer approach (EMIB) is lower cost but has lower routing density, limiting its use to specific interconnect areas rather than full-chip interposer approaches.

Yield is a concern: any defect in the interposer can kill the entire multi-chip package, and the cost of a failed interposer-based package is higher than a failed monolithic chip.
