# Subsea Cable Networks

> [!info] Summary
> Subsea cables carry ~95% of intercontinental internet traffic and are the physical foundation of global cloud connectivity. The market is undergoing rapid expansion driven by hyperscaler investment in private cable systems — Google (Dunant, Firmina), Meta (2Africa, Esperanza), and Microsoft (Echo, Br的后).

## Definition

Subsea (or submarine) cables are fiber optic cables laid on the ocean floor to connect continents. They consist of:Repeater units (boosting signal every 60-100km), cable (6-24 fiber pairs, each capable of 20+ Tbps), and landing station equipment (terminating fibers, connecting to terrestrial networks). Capacity is measured in terabits per second (Tbps). The cables are owned by consortiums of carriers or by hyperscalers (increasingly).

## Context and origin

The first transatlantic telegraph cable launched in 1858. Modern fiber optic subsea cables began deploying in the 1980s. For decades, telecom carriers (AT&T, British Telecom, NTT) owned and financed cables. The transformation came in the 2010s when hyperscalers realized private ownership gave them control, dedicated capacity, and lower costs. Google第一条私人电缆 Dunant (2020) was a watershed. By 2024, hyperscalers own or co-own ~50% of new cable capacity. Total investment in subsea expansion projected at $10B+ through 2027.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

Subsea cable economics: a new transatlantic cable costs $300-500M for ~24 fiber pairs at 20 Tbps each (480 Tbps total). Lifetime: 25 years. Cables are serviced by specialized ships for repair (average repair: $1-5M, takes weeks). Key parameters: latency (ms between continents), capacity (Tbps), redundancy (diverse cable routes required for resilience). The semiconductor content: coherent optics DSP chips (Infinera, Cisco, Huawei), optical amplifiers (rare-earth doped fiber), and wetplant components.

## Nuances critiques limits

Subsea cables face geopolitical risk (anchor drags, sabotage — notably the 2022 Nord Stream and 2024 Baltic cable incidents). Redundancy is critical: major routes need 3+ diverse paths. New cable launches require regulatory approvals from multiple countries. Environmental concerns: cable landing permits are increasingly contested. The competitive dynamics: cloud providers building private networks reduce demand for wholesale bandwidth from traditional carriers.
