# Colocation Real Estate Investment Trusts

> [!info] Summary
> Data center REITs (Digital Realty, Equinix, CyrusOne, Iron Mountain) own and lease physical data center space, power, and connectivity. They provide hyperscalers and enterprises with modular or build-to-suit capacity without owning land or building themselves. Key metrics: contractual backlog, churn, power density per rack, and interconnection density.

## Definition
Colocation real estate investment trusts (REITs) are publicly traded companies that own, develop, and operate data center facilities, renting space, power, and cooling to multiple tenants. Unlikehyperscalers who build and operate their own infrastructure, colocation tenants benefit from shared infrastructure economics while avoiding the capital burden of owning real estate. Retail colocation leases individual cabinets (typically 1-5kW per cabinet) to small and medium enterprises, SaaS providers, and financial services firms needing compliance-grade infrastructure without building their own. Wholesale colocation leases larger footprints — entire pods of 5-20MW or dedicated buildings — to large enterprises, cloud providers establishing availability zones, and government agencies. The REIT structure provides tax advantages (requiring 90% of taxable income distributed to shareholders) while accessing public equity and debt capital markets for expansion.

## Context and Origin
The data center REIT sector emerged in the early 2010s as institutional investors recognized the recurring revenue characteristics of data center leases. Digital Realty (DLR, ticker DLR) was founded in 2004 and went public in 2005, later converting to REIT status in 2007 to access lower-cost financing. Digital Realty is now the largest pure-play data center REIT with over 300 facilities across 25+ countries. Equinix (EQIX) pioneered the carrier-neutral "carrier hotel" model — large interconnection facilities where networks physically meet to exchange traffic — and went public in 2004 before converting to REIT status in 2012. The sector underwent rapid consolidation: Equinix acquired Telecity (2016) and Interxion (2020) to expand European footprint; Digital Realty acquired Interxion (2021) for European scale. CyrusOne (now part of a private consortium after going private in 2022) specialized in wholesale colocation for large enterprises and was acquired by Carlyle Group. Iron Mountain (IRM) expanded from its core records management business into data center services, leveraging its existing real estate relationships.

## Mechanisms, Characteristics, and Details
Data center REITs generate revenue through several mechanisms that provide visibility and growth. Base rent comprises the majority of revenue, typically structured as 3-10 year leases with annual escalation clauses of 2-4% — well above inflation in many markets. Power charges, billed per kilowatt, have become a larger revenue component as AI workloads increase power density. Set-up and installation fees provide one-time revenue when new tenants move in. Key financial metrics for analysis include: annualized co-location revenue (cARR), utilization rates (percentage of available power leased), backlog (signed leases not yet generating revenue), and lease duration-weighted revenue. Fund From Operations (FFO) is the standard REIT metric, adding back depreciation to operating income. Growth drivers include: new data center construction (12-18 month lead time), existing asset expansion (adding power capacity within shell), and acquisitions. Capital allocation priorities typically favor: maintaining dividend coverage, funding organic growth, and pursuing bolt-on acquisitions of smaller operators.

## Nuances, Critiques, and Limitations
Data center REITs offer a unique combination of yield (3-5% dividend yield) and growth (10-15% FFO growth) not found in pure-play REIT sectors. However, they face several structural constraints. Power availability has become the primary limiting factor — major markets (Northern Virginia, Phoenix, Singapore, Amsterdam) are power-constrained, with utility interconnection queues extending 3-5 years in some regions. This power constraint caps deployment speed regardless of demand. New construction requires $200-400M per large-scale facility and 12-18 months for shell completion, creating a lag between demand recognition and revenue generation. Tenant concentration risk is material — the largest REITs have hyperscale customers (AWS, Azure, GCP) representing 15-25% of revenue each, meaning loss of any single major customer impacts financials. Lease renewal rates (typically 80-90% in normal demand environments) can decline during hyperscaler capex pullbacks. For semiconductor investors specifically, data center REITs are indirect beneficiaries of AI infrastructure buildout — they don't sell semiconductors but provide the real estate layer, benefiting when cloud providers expand footprint to accommodate AI chip demand.

## Links and Implications
[[Colocation Real Estate Investment Trusts]] benefit directly from [[Hyperscale Data Center Operators]] expanding their cloud infrastructure — AWS, Azure, and GCP lease millions of square feet from colocation providers to supplement owned facilities. The growth of [[Cloud Infrastructure Market]] (projected to exceed $1T by 2028) creates sustained demand for data center space, driving occupancy and pricing power. [[Data Center Cooling Technologies]] consumption directly impacts REIT operating costs — liquid cooling requirements for AI GPU clusters increase construction costs but also justify higher power pricing. [[Data Center Power Management]] is the most critical operational constraint for colocation operators; power utilization density determines profitability per square foot. [[Subsea Cable Networks]] affect REIT value indirectly: facilities with direct cable landing station access (like Equinix's Miami campus) command premium interconnection pricing. [[Server CPU Market Intel AMD Arm]] indirectly affects REIT demand as chip generations drive server refresh cycles requiring new floor space. The hybrid cloud trend (enterprises maintaining on-premises infrastructure while extending to public cloud) sustains demand for [[On-Premises Data Centers]] migration to colocation facilities, representing a structural tailwind as enterprises exit owned data centers.
