# Colocation Real Estate Investment Trusts

> [!info] Summary
> Data center colocation REITs (Digital Realty, Equinix, CyrusOne, Iron Mountain) own and operate physical data center facilities, renting cabinet space, power, and connectivity to enterprises and cloud providers. The colocation model enables enterprises to avoid the capital expense of building their own data centers while maintaining physical control of their hardware. These REITs benefit from the AI capex boom as hyperscalers lease more power-dense capacity.

## Definition

Colocation data center REITs provide physical space, power, cooling, and network connectivity for customers' IT equipment. Customers lease cabinet space (typically 1-10kW per cabinet), with the REIT handling facility infrastructure (power distribution, cooling, fire suppression, physical security).

Revenue models: retail colocation (small/medium enterprises, variable lease terms), wholesale colocation (hyperscale customers, large dedicated halls, typically 5-15 year leases), and interconnection (cross-connects between customers within a facility).

Key metrics: revenue per watt (total revenue / total installed power capacity), occupancy rate, backlog (signed leases not yet delivering revenue), and leverage ratio (REITs carry significant debt).

## Context and origin

The modern data center REIT sector emerged from the colocation industry of the 1990s. Digital Realty (founded 2004, IPO 2005) pioneered the REIT model for data centers, enabling tax-advantaged real estate ownership for data center assets. Equinix (IPO 2010 as a REIT from 2012) grew to become the global interconnection leader.

The AI boom (2022-present) created a new demand dynamic: GPU clusters require 30-50kW per rack vs 5-10kW for traditional IT, straining power availability at colocation facilities. Hyperscalers increasingly turn to colocation for rapid capacity expansion when hyperscale data center development cannot keep pace with AI demand.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

Data center REITs generate revenue from: base rent (per cabinet per month, typically $100-500/watt-year depending on location and power density), power pass-through (utilities cost, often separately metered), interconnection services (cross-connects, cloud exchanges), and build-to-suit (custom facilities for large tenants).

Power is the key constraint: AI workloads require high power density that older data centers cannot provide. New facilities are being built with 30-50kW per rack capacity, requiring liquid cooling and significant electrical infrastructure investment.

REIT financial structure provides tax advantages ( REIT income passes through to shareholders without corporate taxation if 90%+ distributed as dividends) but requires high dividend payouts, limiting retained capital for growth.

[[Data Center Cooling Technologies]] is directly relevant as higher power densities require advanced cooling (liquid, immersive). [[Data Center Power Management]] covers the electrical infrastructure. [[Hyperscale Data Center Operators]] are key tenants.

## Nuances critiques limits

Data center REITs face geographic concentration risk: Northern Virginia, Dallas, Silicon Valley, and Singapore are the key markets, with supply constrained by power grid availability and zoning. Power grid constraints have become the primary bottleneck to data center expansion in many markets.

The AI workload surge benefits colocation REITs short-term (higher power utilization, premium pricing for high-density deployments) but may drive hyperscalers to build their own facilities if colocation cannot deliver power quickly enough.

REIT leverage is a risk factor: rising interest rates increase debt servicing costs and reduce asset valuations. Data center cap rates have compressed as institutional capital floods the sector.
