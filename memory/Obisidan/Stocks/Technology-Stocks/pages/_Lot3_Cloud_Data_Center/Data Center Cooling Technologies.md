# Data Center Cooling Technologies

> [!info] Summary
> AI GPU clusters consume 80-120kW per rack vs 10-30kW for traditional CPU servers, demanding revolutionary cooling approaches: liquid cooling (direct-to-chip, immersion), raised floor containment, and waste heat recovery. Key players: Vertiv, Schneider Electric, Rittal, and emerging liquid cooling specialists like ZutaCore and Iceotope.

## Definition
Data center cooling removes heat generated by servers, networking equipment, and storage systems to maintain operational temperatures within acceptable ranges. Traditional cooling approaches employ computer room air conditioners (CRAC) and computer room air handlers (CRAH) using chilled water systems or direct expansion (DX) refrigeration. These air-based systems can scale to approximately 30kW per rack through large fan coils and raised floor airflow management. However, AI GPU clusters from NVIDIA (H100, H200, GB200) generate heat at 10x the density of traditional CPU servers, fundamentally breaking the economics of air cooling at scale. Direct liquid cooling (DLC) brings chilled water directly to server racks through manifolds, while cold plate technology attaches cooling plates directly to processors. Immersion cooling submerges entire servers in dielectric fluid — either single-phase (constant liquid form) or two-phase (fluid boils on contact with hot components).

## Context and Origin
The evolution of data center cooling tracks the density trajectory of compute hardware. Early mainframes in the 1960s-70s required precision air conditioning comparable to surgical environments. The rise of enterprise servers in the 1990s normalized raised-floor data centers with CRAH units drawing cold air from underfloor plenum. By the 2000s, hypervisors and virtualization consolidated workloads, increasing utilization but also power density. The AI revolution beginning ~2012 with AlexNet and accelerating through GPT models created a step-change in power requirements. NVIDIA's H100 GPU consumes 700W per chip, and a single DGX H100 system contains eight GPUs drawing 7.2kW. The GB200 NVL72 rack-scale system consumes over 120kW for the GPUs alone, excluding server infrastructure — necessitating direct liquid cooling as the only viable thermal solution. Industry analysts estimate that by 2027, over 60% of new data center deployments will require some form of liquid cooling.

## Mechanisms, Characteristics, and Details
The cooling market includes several distinct technology segments with different competitive dynamics. Air cooling remains relevant for edge deployments and low-density traditional workloads — manufacturers like Schneider Electric and Vertiv continue to innovate in CRAH efficiency through variable speed fans and AI-driven airflow optimization. Direct liquid cooling (DLC) connects chilled water supply (typically 45-50°F) from central plants directly to rack-level cooling manifolds. The water loops through cold plates mounted on processors, absorbing heat directly where it is generated. Key DLC component suppliers include Smyrna (cold plates), AFC (quick-disconnect fittings), and major server OEMs designing DLC integration. Immersion cooling companies like Submer (Spain), GRC (US), and LiQuiBit (Taiwan) offer complete solutions for high-density deployments — Submer's MetaLoop system is deployed at scale by Microsoft. Rear-door heat exchangers attach to the rear of racks and use water coils to capture heat without modifying server hardware, representing a transitional technology. Thermacore and other thermal interface material (TIM) suppliers provide the critical compounds ensuring proper heat transfer between chips and cooling hardware.

## Nuances, Critiques, and Limitations
Cooling infrastructure represents a direct beneficiary of AI compute density growth, but the market is not uniformly exposed. Traditional air cooling manufacturers face declining relevance in hyperscale AI deployments while gaining share in enterprise edge locations. Vertiv, the largest pure-play cooling infrastructure company, went public via SPAC in 2021 and has since seen its market cap increase substantially as data center power density became a critical constraint. However, liquid cooling systems require more complex maintenance than air systems, with water leakage risks requiring sophisticated monitoring and detection. The water consumption of direct liquid cooling is significant — a large hyperscale data center can use millions of gallons daily — raising sustainability concerns in water-stressed regions. Cooling tower operation consumes substantial power itself, representing 10-15% of total data center energy use in air-cooled facilities. The shift to liquid cooling drives demand for specialized components including cold plates, quick-disconnect fittings, heat exchangers, and advanced thermal interface materials from suppliers like Bergquist (now part of Parker Hannifin).

## Links and Implications
[[Data Center Cooling Technologies]] is inseparable from [[Data Center Power Management]] — both address the fundamental constraint of delivering and dissipating energy in dense compute environments. The GB200 NVL72's thermal challenges are directly tied to [[Power Semiconductor Packaging]] advances, as more efficient power delivery reduces heat generation at the source. [[Hyperscale Data Center Operators]] including Google, Microsoft, and Amazon are the primary deployers of advanced cooling, driving demand for [[Advanced Packaging Technologies]] that enable higher power chips with manageable thermal profiles. Cooling towers and water-intensive cooling methods create significant demand signals for companies in [[Water Management Fabs]] and related water infrastructure. [[Server CPU Market Intel AMD Arm]] is affected by cooling: AMD's EPYC processors require different thermal solutions than Intel Xeon, and Arm-based server chips from Ampere and Amazon's Graviton series present unique cooling requirements. The modular data center trend (see [[Vertiv Holdings]]) enables liquid-cooled pods that can be deployed faster than traditional construction. For investors, Vertiv (VRT) and Schneider Electric (SU) represent pure-play exposure to data center cooling capex growth, while HVAC majors like Carrier (CARR) benefit from peripheral cooling demand.
