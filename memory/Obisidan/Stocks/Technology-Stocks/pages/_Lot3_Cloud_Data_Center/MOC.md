# Lot 3: Cloud & Data Center — Map of Concepts

> [!info] Overview
> 10 pages covering cloud infrastructure, hyperscale operators, server components, networking, power, cooling, and edge computing.

## Pages

| Page | Summary |
|------|---------|
| [[Cloud Infrastructure Market]] | The global cloud infrastructure market reached $500B+ in 2023, growing 15-20% annually. AWS leads with ~32% share, Azure ~23%, GCP ~10%. Hyperscalers compete aggressively on AI capabilities, driving unprecedented AI infrastructure capex. |
| [[Hyperscale Data Center Operators]] | Hyperscale operators (AWS, Azure, GCP, Meta, Microsoft, Apple) operate massive data centers with 100K+ servers globally. Their capex decisions drive demand for servers, networking, cooling, and power equipment — making them the primary customer for AI accelerator and semiconductor suppliers. |
| [[Data Center Networking Chips]] | Data center networking chips enable high-speed communication between servers in AI training clusters. Key products: Broadcom's Tomahawk/Jericho chips, Marvell's Teralynx, Nvidia's Mellanox InfiniBand. AI clusters require 400G/800G Ethernet and InfiniBand for multi-GPU communication. |
| [[Data Center Power Management]] | Data centers consume 1-2% of global electricity, with AI training clusters demanding 10-100x more power per rack than traditional compute. Power delivery, conversion, and backup systems represent $30B+ annual market — creating demand for power semiconductors (SiC, GaN, IGBT) and power management chips. |
| [[Server CPU Market Intel AMD Arm]] | The server CPU market is ~$40B annually. Intel still leads in x86 server market share (~70%) but AMD EPYC has gained significantly. AWS Graviton (Arm) is growing rapidly — Amazon designs its own Arm-based CPUs for cost and efficiency, representing a structural threat to both Intel and AMD. |
| [[Cloud Storage Technologies]] | Cloud storage encompasses block, file, and object storage delivered as a service. Object storage (S3, Azure Blob, GCS) dominates for unstructured data at exabyte scale. Block storage (EBS, managed databases) provides low-latency access for transactional workloads. Key semiconductor content: NAND controllers, DRAM, and emerging computational storage. |
| [[Data Center Cooling Technologies]] | AI GPU clusters consume 80-120kW per rack vs 10-30kW for traditional CPU servers, demanding revolutionary cooling approaches: liquid cooling (direct-to-chip, immersion), raised floor containment, and waste heat recovery. Key players: Vertiv, Schneider Electric, Rittal, and emerging liquid cooling specialists like ZutaCore and Iceotope. |
| [[Colocation Real Estate Investment Trusts]] | Data center REITs (Digital Realty, Equinix, CyrusOne, Iron Mountain) own and lease physical data center space, power, and connectivity. They provide hyperscalers and enterprises with modular or build-to-suit capacity without owning land or building themselves. Key metrics: contractual backlog, churn, power density per rack, and interconnection density. |
| [[Edge Computing Infrastructure]] | Edge computing pushes computation from centralized cloud data centers to the network edge — telecom central offices, on-premise servers, and IoT gateways. It addresses latency (5-20ms vs 80-120ms round-trip to public cloud), bandwidth costs, and data sovereignty requirements. |
| [[Subsea Cable Networks]] | Subsea cables carry ~95% of intercontinental internet traffic and are the physical foundation of global cloud connectivity. The market is undergoing rapid expansion driven by hyperscaler investment in private cable systems — Google (Dunant, Firmina), Meta (2Africa, Esperanza), and Microsoft (Echo, Br的后). |
