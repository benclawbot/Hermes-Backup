# Apple Silicon Mobile Strategy

> [!info] Summary
> Apple's in-house silicon strategy for mobile devices (iPhone, iPad, Apple Watch) has delivered performance and efficiency leadership through tight hardware-software integration. Starting with the A7 chip in iPhone 5s (2013), Apple has progressed through the A-series (A14, A15, A16, A17 Pro) with custom CPU cores, GPU cores, Neural Engine, and ISP, achieving leadership over Qualcomm and MediaTek competitors.

## Definition

Apple designs custom application processors for iPhone (A-series), iPad (A-series and M-series), and Apple Watch (S-series and W-series). These chips are manufactured exclusively at TSMC using Apple's reserved foundry capacity. Apple's A-series chips have featured custom CPU cores starting with A7 (Cyclone, 2013), custom GPU cores starting with A11 Bionic (2017), and the Neural Engine starting with A11 Bionic.

Apple's strategy differs from other ARM licensees: Apple has an ARM Architectural License, meaning it can design its own ARM-compatible instruction set implementations rather than using ARM's preset Cortex cores. This has allowed Apple to achieve performance leadership.

## Context and origin

Apple's A7 (2013) was the first 64-bit ARM smartphone processor, surprising the industry by leapfrogging competitors. The subsequent A8, A9, A10, and A11 Bionic built on this lead with increasing customization. Apple's A12 Bionic (2018) maintained leadership; the A14 Bionic (2020) was the first 5nm smartphone chip; the A17 Pro (2023) is manufactured at 3nm.

The Apple Watch S-series chips (starting 2015) are more modest in performance but optimized for the Watch's small battery form factor. The W-series (W1, W2, W3) are wireless chips for AirPods and Beats headphones.

## Nuances critiques limits

Apple's captive approach means it cannot purchase chips from Qualcomm or MediaTek, limiting competitive tension in iPhone specifications. Apple's proprietary approach also means its chip development costs must be absorbed internally rather than spread across the industry.

The in-house modem development (expected to debut ~2025) represents the final step in Apple's vertical integration of core silicon components. This has significant implications for [[Qualcomm Business Analysis]] as Apple reduces its Qualcomm modem purchases.
