# 5G Mobile Infrastructure

> [!info] Summary
> 5G mobile infrastructure buildout drives smartphone upgrade cycles globally, with the US, China, South Korea, and Europe leading deployment. RAN equipment suppliers (Ericsson, Nokia, Samsung) and semiconductor suppliers (Qualcomm, Intel, Marvell) benefit from the infrastructure buildout. The mmWave 5G rollout in the US has been slower than expected due to coverage challenges, while sub-6GHz 5G has been broadly deployed.

## Definition

5G networks use two frequency ranges: sub-6GHz (600MHz to 6GHz, primarily 3.5GHz) providing broad coverage similar to 4G LTE, and mmWave (24GHz to 100GHz) providing very high bandwidth but limited coverage (100-200m per small cell).

5G infrastructure components: gNodeB (5G base station), AAU (Active Antenna Unit with integrated radio), DU (Distributed Unit) processing baseband, CU (Central Unit) for higher-layer protocols, and backhaul connectivity to the core network.

## Context and origin

5G was standardized by 3GPP in Release 15 (2018), with initial commercial deployments in 2019. The US led initial mmWave deployments (Verizon), while South Korea and China led sub-6GHz deployments. By 2024, sub-6GHz 5G coverage is broadly available in most developed markets.

The COVID-19 pandemic slightly delayed some infrastructure deployments but may have accelerated some government programs to expand broadband connectivity.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The network effect: more 5G infrastructure drives smartphone 5G adoption, which drives more carrier investment in 5G infrastructure. This virtuous cycle has driven the fastest 5G adoption of any cellular generation.

The link to [[Cellular Baseband Processors]] is important: baseband chips in infrastructure (small cells, macro cells) represent a different market from smartphone basebands. The link to [[Network Security Appliances]] connects to the security infrastructure that must evolve alongside 5G.

## Nuances critiques limits

The mmWave business case has been disappointing. Verizon's mmWave 5G deployments have covered relatively limited areas (stadiums, airports, downtown cores) and most consumers cannot distinguish mmWave 5G from sub-6GHz 5G in practice.

The Open RAN (O-RAN) initiative is creating competitive disruption for traditional RAN equipment suppliers (Ericsson, Nokia). O-RAN specifies open interfaces between RU (Radio Unit), DU, and CU, potentially allowing mix-and-match hardware from different suppliers.
