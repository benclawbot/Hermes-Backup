# CPU Market Intel AMD ARM

> [!info] Summary
> The CPU market for PCs and servers is transitioning from Intel's historical dominance to a three-way competition between Intel (x86), AMD (x86), and ARM-based processors (Qualcomm Snapdragon X, Apple M-series, NVIDIA Grace). The server CPU market is the highest-value battleground, while the PC market is experiencing ARM's most significant x86 challenge in 2024-2025.

## Definition

The CPU market for personal computing includes: desktop PC processors, notebook PC processors, and server processors. The market is currently undergoing a significant architecture transition as ARM-based processors (Apple Silicon, Snapdragon X Elite, and others) challenge x86 (Intel and AMD) in Windows PCs for the first time at scale.

Intel pioneered the x86 architecture in 1978 and has been the dominant PC and server CPU vendor for four decades. AMD's Ryzen architecture (2017) ended Intel's single-source dominance in PC CPUs. ARM architecture has been used in smartphones for 15+ years and is now attempting to move up the value chain into PCs and servers.

## Context and origin

Intel's PC CPU dominance was challenged by AMD's Opteron in servers (2003) and later by AMD's Ryzen in PCs (2017). The competitive dynamic between Intel and AMD has been the central narrative of the PC CPU market for two decades.

The more disruptive transition is ARM's move into PCs. Apple's transition from Intel to Apple Silicon (M1 in 2020) demonstrated that ARM-based PCs could match or exceed x86 performance at lower power. Microsoft's Windows on ARM (with Snapdragon X Elite starting 2024) is the next step in this transition.

## Nuances critiques limits

The ARM transition for Windows PCs is still early. Snapdragon X Elite (2024) has received positive reviews but faces the challenge of Windows x86 application compatibility (handled through emulation, which has performance costs). Enterprise adoption of ARM Windows PCs will be slow due to software compatibility concerns.

The server CPU market is also transitioning more slowly to ARM than the PC market. Amazon's Graviton, Ampere's Altra, and NVIDIA's Grace are ARM server CPUs, but Intel and AMD still dominate server CPU deployments.
