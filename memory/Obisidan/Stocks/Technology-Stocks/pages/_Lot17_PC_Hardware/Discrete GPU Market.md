# Discrete GPU Market

> [!info] Summary
> The discrete GPU market is a two-player duopoly of NVIDIA (80%+ market share by revenue) and AMD (sub-20%), serving gaming, professional visualization, and AI inference markets. The market experienced a cryptocurrency-driven demand surge in 2020-2021 followed by a normalization, with AI compute demand now providing a structural demand floor above prior cyclical peaks.

## Definition

Discrete GPUs are separate graphics cards (add-in boards) that connect to a PC via PCIe slot, as opposed to integrated GPUs embedded in the CPU die. Discrete GPUs contain their own memory (VRAM), compute units, and cooling solutions, enabling significantly higher performance than integrated graphics.

Market segments: gaming (largest by unit volume), professional visualization (workstation GPUs for CAD, rendering), and AI inference/machine learning (using GPU tensor cores for inference workloads). The AI inference market emerged as a significant demand driver starting around 2018.

## Context and origin

The discrete GPU market consolidated from three major players (3dfx, NVIDIA, ATI) in the late 1990s to two (NVIDIA, AMD after acquiring ATI in 2006). Intel entered the discrete GPU market with Arc graphics in 2022, but has yet to achieve meaningful market share.

NVIDIA's CUDA ecosystem (launched 2006) was the key strategic advantage that allowed NVIDIA to dominate AI applications. AMD's ROCm open compute platform has made inroads but CUDA remains the preferred AI development environment.

## Nuances critiques limits

The crypto demand volatility has distorted the market twice (2017-2018 and 2020-2021). While AI demand now provides a structural floor, the gaming GPU market remains subject to consumer discretionary spending cycles.

Intel's Arc discrete GPUs represent a third competitive threat, but Intel's driver maturity and software ecosystem issues have limited adoption. If Intel succeeds long-term, the duopoly structure would change.

The used GPU market (from cryptocurrency mining) creates secondary market competition that depresses new GPU pricing during demand downturns.
