# DRAM Memory Market

> [!info] Summary
> DRAM is the workhorse memory of computing, providing fast, affordable, volatile storage for active data in smartphones, PCs, servers, and embedded systems. The DRAM market is a tight oligopoly of Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron that has historically exhibited extreme cyclicality, withboom-bust price cycles driven by capacity discipline or the lack thereof, and currently undergoing structural changes from AI training demand that is reshaping bit growth and ASP dynamics.

## Definition

Dynamic RAM (DRAM) stores each bit as a charge on a capacitor that must be periodically refreshed. DRAM is volatile memory — data is lost when power is removed. It is the primary system memory in all computing devices, providing the fast random access that CPUs require. DRAM performance is characterized by bandwidth (GB/s), capacity per chip (Gb), and power consumption.

The three main application segments are: mobile DRAM (low-power LPDDR used in smartphones and laptops), PC DRAM (DDR4/DDR5 for desktops and laptops), and server DRAM (DDR5 for data centers, the highest-margin segment).

## Context and origin

DRAM was invented by Robert Dennard at IBM in 1966 and commercialized by Intel in 1970. The industry went through multiple waves of competition: US pioneers (Intel, Mostek, AMD) were displaced by Japanese manufacturers (NEC, Toshiba, Hitachi) in the 1980s, who were then displaced by Korean manufacturers (Samsung, LG, Hynix) in the 1990s. By the 2000s, the market had consolidated to Samsung, Hynix, and later Micron (via acquisition of Elpida in 2013).

The 2020s have seen a dramatic shift: the previous cycle of cyclical boom-bust (2017-2019 oversupply, 2020-2022 shortage) has given way to a more complex picture where AI demand for HBM (high-bandwidth memory, a specialized DRAM product) is creating structural tightness even as standard DRAM remains cyclical.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The [[Hynix Memory Business]] and [[Samsung Memory Business]] pages cover the manufacturer competitive dynamics. The key industry dynamics include:

Capacity discipline: DRAM manufacturers have historically had poor capacity discipline, adding capacity during price upcycles that eventually causes oversupply and price collapse. The 2019 DRAM downturn was severe, with prices falling approximately 70% from peak.

HBM demand transformation: AI training requires HBM, a specialized DRAM product stacked in 3D configurations with wide interfaces. HBM commands significant ASP and margin premiums over standard DRAM. SK Hynix is the HBM leader (supplying HBM3 to NVIDIA), with Samsung and Micron racing to catch up.

The link to [[Cloud Infrastructure Spending]] is direct: hyperscalers are the primary server DRAM buyers, and their capex cycles drive DRAM demand. The link to [[AI Accelerator Chips]] is emerging as AI training demand drives HBM consumption.

## Nuances critiques limits

The AI transformation of DRAM is not uniform. Standard DDR5 server DRAM remains cyclical and faces oversupply risk. Only the HBM segment benefits from structural demand growth, and not all DRAM manufacturers can produce HBM at competitive quality/yields.

Samsung's manufacturing capacity advantage is significant but its HBM development lagged SK Hynix, creating a strategic vulnerability. Samsung's loss of HBM leadership to Hynix in the current AI cycle is unusual — Samsung has historically been the technology leader in DRAM.

The China risk: CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies) is a Chinese DRAM manufacturer attempting to build domestic DRAM capability. CXMT remains years behind the leaders in process technology but represents a long-term competitive threat if Chinese government support continues.
