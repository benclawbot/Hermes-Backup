# Samsung Memory Business

> [!info] Summary
> Samsung Electronics' memory semiconductor division (Samsung Memory) is the world's largest manufacturer of DRAM and NAND flash memory, representing one of Samsung Group's most strategically important businesses. Samsung Memory's scale, technology leadership, and integration with Samsung's broader electronics business make it the industry's benchmark competitor, though it has lost its HBM leadership to SK Hynix in the current AI cycle.

## Definition

Samsung Memory produces DRAM chips (DDR4, DDR5, LPDDR, HBM) and NAND flash chips (3D V-NAND) sold both internally to Samsung Electronics divisions and externally to other electronics manufacturers. The division is part of Samsung Electronics' Device Solutions business, which also includes foundry and display businesses.

The company operates memory fabs in South Korea (Giheung, Hwaseong, Pyeongtaek) and has additional capacity in China (Xi'an). Samsung is the only memory manufacturer that is truly integrated across the entire electronics supply chain, supplying memory to competitors that also compete with Samsung in end products.

## Context and origin

Samsung Electronics entered DRAM manufacturing in the early 1990s, and by the mid-1990s had become the world's leading DRAM producer through aggressive capital investment and manufacturing optimization. The company maintained DRAM leadership through the 2000s and 2010s, using DRAM profits to fund its foundry and display expansion.

Samsung pioneered 3D NAND with V-NAND, first commercializing it in 2013. The vertical channel approach allowed Samsung to continue density scaling when planar NAND was hitting limits, maintaining cost leadership.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

Samsung's memory business benefits from scale, vertical integration, and cross-subsidization. Samsung can afford to run its memory business at lower margins than standalone competitors because the memory profits fund strategic investments in foundry (to compete with TSMC) and display (to compete with LG/BOE).

The current HBM challenge is significant. Samsung has historically been the technology leader in memory, but SK Hynix surpassed Samsung in HBM. Samsung lost the HBM3 qualification for NVIDIA's H100, which is a major strategic setback. Samsung is now racing to close the HBM3E gap and win future GPU qualifications.

Samsung Memory's [[NAND Flash Memory]] and [[DRAM Memory Market]] position is #1 globally. The [[Solid State Drives]] market benefits Samsung because its integrated SSD products (using Samsung NAND and controllers) compete with standalone SSD controller vendors.

## Nuances critiques limits

Samsung's memory business is ultimately a political entity within Samsung Group, not purely a profit-maximizing business. The foundry ambition (competing with TSMC) means Samsung sometimes makes strategic decisions about memory capacity that are not purely economically rational.

The HBM loss to Hynix is particularly notable because Samsung has never before been second in any major memory technology. The close collaboration between Hynix and NVIDIA on HBM co-design may have created a switching cost that Samsung cannot easily overcome.

Samsung's cross-subsidization model means its memory division may not always make independent capacity decisions, potentially distorting the supply-demand balance in ways standalone companies cannot.
