> [!info] Summary
> The semiconductor industry designs and manufactures the chips that power all modern computing, with the sector generating over $600B in annual revenue and serving as a critical bottleneck in the global technology supply chain.

## Definition

The semiconductor industry encompasses the design, fabrication, packaging, and testing of integrated circuits (ICs) and microchips that form the foundation of all modern electronic systems. At its core, the industry transforms raw silicon wafers into billions of transistors and complex circuit architectures through a series of capital-intensive manufacturing processes. The industry splits broadly into three segments: integrated device manufacturers (IDMs) like Samsung and Intel that handle both design and fabrication in-house, fabless companies like NVIDIA and AMD that design chips but outsource manufacturing to foundries, and pure-play foundries like TSMC that focus exclusively on fabrication services for external customers.

Key players in the ecosystem include TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) which commands roughly 60% of the foundry market, Samsung Electronics which competes at advanced nodes, and ASML Holding which maintains a monopolistic position in extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography systems essential for leading-edge chip production. The revenue breakdown shows logic semiconductors (processors, controllers) representing approximately 40% of total industry revenue, memory semiconductors (DRAM, NAND) accounting for roughly 25%, and analog, sensors, and other specialized chips making up the remaining 35%. The industry operates as a critical bottleneck in global technology supply chains, with geographic concentration in Taiwan (producing over 60% of logic chips) and South Korea creating strategic vulnerabilities for downstream industries from automotive to data centers.

## Context and origin

The semiconductor industry's origins trace to 1958 when Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments and Robert Noyce at Fairchild Semiconductor independently invented the integrated circuit, marking the beginning of silicon-based electronics. The subsequent decades saw the emergence of Silicon Valley as the epicenter of innovation, with pioneers like Gordon Moore formulating the observation (later known as Moore's Law) that transistor density would double approximately every two years. The 1970s and 1980s witnessed the rise of the PC revolution, where Intel's x86 processors and Microsoft's software ecosystem defined computing's trajectory for decades, while memory chip manufacturing shifted to Japan and later South Korea through aggressive industrial policy.

The mobile computing era beginning with smartphones in the late 2000s fundamentally restructured the industry, demanding low-power, application-specific chips that favored ARM-based architectures and specialized AI accelerators. Foundries like TSMC emerged as the manufacturing backbone for fabless companies, enabling the fabless model that decoupled chip design from capital-intensive fabrication. The current AI computing wave, accelerating from roughly 2017 onward, has created explosive demand for high-bandwidth memory (HBM), advanced logic chips, and custom accelerators, driving unprecedented capital expenditure cycles. Each era brought market cycles—sharp downturns in 1985, 1995, 2001, 2008, and 2019—that tested industry participants' resilience while rewarding those with sustained technological leadership.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The semiconductor value chain flows from electronic design automation (EDA) tools provided by companies like Synopsys and Cadence, through chip design by fabless firms or IDMs, to wafer fabrication at advanced semiconductor fabs requiring multibillion-dollar cleanroom facilities. The fabrication process involves hundreds of sequential steps including deposition, lithography, etching, and ion implantation, with leading-edge processes requiring ASML's EUV machines capable of printing transistors at 3nm and below. Following wafer fabrication, chips undergo packaging and test, where advanced packaging techniques like TSMC's CoWoS and Samsung's X-Cube enable heterogeneous integration of logic, memory, and other components into system-in-package configurations.

The industry exhibits extreme capital intensity, with a leading-edge fab costing $15-20 billion to construct and requiring years to reach yield targets. R&D cycles stretch 5-10 years for fundamental process technology, creating high barriers to entry. Fab utilization economics heavily influence profitability—fabs running below 80% utilization face severe margin compression since fixed costs (depreciation, maintenance, personnel) dominate the cost structure. TSMC's foundry model, pioneered in 1987, fundamentally changed the industry by allowing design companies to access leading-edge manufacturing without building their own fabs, enabling the fabless ecosystem that produced NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm, and countless others. ASML's monopoly on EUV lithography represents a critical chokepoint, with its machines requiring 40+ shipping containers per unit and relying on supply chains spanning thousands of components from hundreds of specialized suppliers.

## Nuances critiques limits

The semiconductor industry faces significant cybersecurity and supply chain security concerns, as chips serve as potential vectors for hardware trojans or malicious modifications that could compromise national security or corporate infrastructure. Geopolitical risks loom large, particularly around the Taiwan Strait where TSMC operates its most advanced fabs—if geopolitical tensions escalated, the resulting supply disruption would cripple global technology supply chains within months, with no viable alternative at scale for leading-edge production. The cyclicality of memory markets (DRAM and NAND) creates boom-bust patterns where excessive capital spending during upcycles leads to overcapacity, price crashes, and dramatic profit swings for Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron.

The manufacturing concentration in East Asia represents both an economic efficiency and a strategic vulnerability, with Taiwan alone responsible for over 90% of advanced logic chip production and most advanced packaging capabilities. Environmental concerns compound these issues—fabs consume millions of gallons of ultra-pure water daily, require exotic chemical precursors with disposal challenges, and demand enormous electricity loads that strain regional power grids. The industry's fundamental tension between globalization's efficiency and resilience remains unresolved, with efforts to build alternative supply chains in the US, Europe, and Japan requiring decade-long timelines and billions in subsidies that may prove insufficient to match Asian cost structures and expertise concentrations.
