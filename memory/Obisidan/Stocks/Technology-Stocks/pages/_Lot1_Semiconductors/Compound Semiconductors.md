# Compound Semiconductors

> [!info] Summary
> Compound semiconductors combine elements from multiple columns of the periodic table — typically III-V compounds like gallium arsenide (GaAs), gallium nitride (GaN), and indium phosphide (InP). These materials offer advantages over silicon including higher electron mobility, wider bandgap, and ability to emit light, making them essential for RF power, LED lighting, photonics, and high-frequency applications.

## Definition

Compound semiconductors are materials formed from two or more elements, typically from groups III and V of the periodic table (III-V compounds). The most commercially significant are gallium arsenide (GaAs), gallium nitride (GaN), indium phosphide (InP), and silicon carbide (SiC). Unlike silicon — which dominates conventional digital logic — compound semiconductors offer distinct electrical properties: higher electron mobility (enabling faster switching), wider bandgaps (supporting higher voltages and temperatures), and direct bandgaps (allowing efficient light emission). These characteristics make III-V materials indispensable for applications where silicon reaches its physical limits.

From an investment perspective, compound semiconductors occupy niche but strategically critical positions in the semiconductor value chain. The market is much smaller than silicon (~$10B vs ~$600B total semiconductor revenue) but grows from AI infrastructure, electric vehicles, and 5G deployment. Key producers include Wolfspeed (SiC), Qorvo and Skyworks Solutions (GaAs RF), and Cree (now Wolfspeed) — alongside vertically integrated players like Samsung Electronics and TSMC that incorporate compound materials in specialized processes.

## Context and origin

The compound semiconductor industry emerged alongside silicon but developed along parallel, specialized tracks. GaAs was first used in microwave and RF applications during the 1970s and 1980s, driven by defense and telecommunications needs that silicon bipolar transistors could not address. The GaAs RF power amplifier became the backbone of cellular infrastructure — every smartphone contains GaAs RF front-end modules handling the wireless signal. GaN entered commercial production in the early 2000s, initially for LED lighting (which accounts for the largest volume of GaN usage today) and subsequently for RF power and power electronics.

The industry's structure differs fundamentally from silicon. Rather than a few dominant players controlling hundreds of billions of dollars of capacity, compound semiconductors remain served by a fragmented ecosystem of specialty foundries and integrated players. This reflects the lower total market size, the diverse materials science expertise required, and the custom nature of compound semiconductor processes. Companies like GlobalFoundries, Win Semiconductor, and UMS Semiconductor operate dedicated GaAs/GaN fabs, while silicon CMOS fabs like TSMC and Samsung have added compound materials to their heterogeneous packaging offerings.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The performance advantages of compound semiconductors stem from their superior electronic properties compared to silicon. GaAs offers electron mobility approximately 5x higher than silicon, enabling faster transistor operation at equivalent geometries — beneficial for RF and microwave frequencies. GaN's wide bandgap (3.4 eV vs silicon's 1.1 eV) allows operation at voltages exceeding 600V with minimal conduction losses, making it ideal for power electronics in electric vehicles and industrial applications. InP's direct bandgap enables efficient light emission at wavelengths used in fiber optic communications (1310nm and 1550nm).

For RF power applications — the largest commercial segment — GaAs RF power amplifiers dominate smartphone transmit chains and cellular base station infrastructure. Each smartphone contains one to four GaAs RF power amplifier modules, representing hundreds of millions of units annually. Qorvo and Skyworks Solutions control the majority of this market, competing on efficiency (battery life) and integration (multi-band support). The transition to 5G has increased RF complexity, requiring more GaAs content per device as multiple frequency bands must be supported simultaneously.

GaN-on-SiC power electronics represent the fastest-growing segment, driven by electric vehicle onboard chargers, inverters, and server power supplies. The superior thermal conductivity of SiC substrates (approaching diamond) allows GaN-on-SiC devices to operate at junction temperatures that silicon MOSFETs cannot sustain. Wolfspeed (formerly Cree) is the market leader in SiC substrates, while competitors like onsemi and STMicroelectronics are investing heavily in SiC capacity. The EV market alone could require $2-5B of additional SiC wafer capacity by 2030 according to industry estimates.

## Nuances critiques limits

The compound semiconductor industry faces three structural limitations. First, the market is small relative to silicon, making it insufficiently attractive for massive capital deployment — new capacity additions are modest and incremental rather than the decade-defining fab builds characteristic of leading-edge silicon. Second, compound semiconductor processes are less standardized than silicon CMOS — each foundry tends to have proprietary variations in epitaxial growth, lithography, and passivation that create switching costs for fabless chip companies. Third, silicon CMOS continues to erode the addressable market: RF CMOS has displaced GaAs in some low-power RF applications, and silicon power MOSFETs compete with GaN in lower-voltage power conversion.

Geopolitical risks concentrate in substrate supply. Wolfspeed's dominance in SiC substrates gives it leverage over the emerging EV power electronics market — if SiC wafer supply tightens, EV OEM pricing and margins face pressure. Chinese competitors (Sanan Optoelectronics, ET Technologies) are building SiC capacity but remain behind leading players in substrate quality. For GaAs, the substrate supply chain is relatively diversified across US, European, and Asian producers.
