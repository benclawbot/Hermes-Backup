# Fabless Competition and Pricing Power

> [!info] Summary
> The fabless semiconductor industry exhibits strong winner-take-most dynamics in large addressable markets but fragmentation in smaller ones, with pricing power varying significantly by competitive position and customer concentration. Understanding where individual fabless companies sit in this hierarchy is essential to evaluating their margin durability and capital allocation flexibility.

## Definition

Fabless semiconductor companies compete in markets ranging from near-monopolies to highly fragmented commodity segments. Pricing power in this industry depends on three structural factors: the uniqueness of the product (can customers easily switch?), the size and concentration of the customer base (does one or two buyers dominate?), and the pace of technology change (does the product need frequent replacement?).

These factors vary dramatically across segments. AI accelerators currently have high pricing power because supply is constrained and switching costs are enormous. Mobile SoCs have moderate pricing power because customers (Apple, Samsung, Chinese brands) are powerful but the technology is differentiated. Memory-interface chips have low pricing power because they are relatively standardized.

## Context and origin

The [[Fabless Semiconductor Model]] emerged as a way to decouple design from manufacturing, but the competitive dynamics within fabless have always been uneven. Some segments developed winner-take-most structures (NVIDIA in AI GPUs), while others remained brutal commodity markets (mobile baseband in the feature-phone era).

The explosion of AI demand since 2022 created the most dramatic shift in fabless pricing power in decades. NVIDIA's H100 and H200 GPUs sold at prices well above list because hyperscalers competed for allocation. This pricing power was unusual in the semiconductor industry and rare for a merchant vendor with alternatives available. Understanding why it happened illuminates the broader pattern.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

NVIDIA's AI pricing power comes from three reinforcing advantages: hardware leadership (no competitor offers equivalent training performance), software ecosystem lock-in (CUDA makes switching prohibitive), and supply constraint (TSMC capacity for advanced CoWoS packaging is limited). See [[NVIDIA Business Analysis]] for details on how these factors combine.

[[Qualcomm Business Analysis]] and [[Broadcom Business Analysis]] operate in markets with moderate pricing power. High switching costs and technology differentiation protect margins, but large customers like Apple and major hyperscalers can extract price concessions because they have real alternatives. The baseband licensing business has historically had very high pricing power because the patents are standards-essential and difficult to design around.

[[MediaTek Business Analysis]] competes in lower-pricing-power segments of mobile, where the availability of competitive alternatives from multiple vendors limits ASP and margin expansion. This is why MediaTek's strategy focuses on volume and cost reduction rather than premium pricing.

Customer concentration is a double-edged sword. Large customers (Apple buying modems, Amazon buying Graviton) can demand lower prices, but they also need the product badly enough that they cannot easily walk away. The result is a negotiated price somewhere between monopoly rents and competitive commodity pricing.

The foundry relationship cuts both ways on pricing. [[TSMC Competitive Position]] gives foundries significant pricing power over fabless companies — TSMC raised prices in 2022-2023 and absorbed some margin at the expense of fabless customers. However, fabless companies with sufficient volume and strategic importance can negotiate preferential terms.

## Nuances critiques limits

A key nuance is that pricing power is dynamic. A company with strong pricing power can lose it if a competitor closes the technology gap or a customer successfully diversifies supply. The AI accelerator market is particularly fluid right now, with custom silicon from hyperscalers threatening NVIDIA's position.

Another issue is that high pricing power in semiconductors often reflects a temporary structural advantage that erodes as the market matures and competitors invest. The companies that sustain pricing power longest are those that keep advancing technology faster than customers can develop alternatives.

Finally, pricing power and volume can trade off. A company maximizing margin per unit may sacrifice volume, while a volume-focused competitor can sometimes dominate market share. The right strategy depends on the market structure and the company's financial position.
