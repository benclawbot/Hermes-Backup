# Fabless Semiconductor Model

> [!info] Summary
> The fabless model separates chip design from wafer fabrication, allowing companies to focus on R&D and product architecture while outsourcing manufacturing to dedicated foundries such as TSMC. This model enabled the semiconductor industry's explosion of diversity and innovation, lowered capital barriers for chip companies, and concentrated manufacturing expertise in a few elite facilities.

## Definition

A fabless semiconductor company designs and markets chips but does not own or operate wafer fabrication facilities. Instead, it taps external foundries to manufacture its designs. The fabless company retains all intellectual property, product roadmaps, and sales while outsourcing the enormous capital and operational costs of building and running leading-edge fabs.

The model contrasts with integrated device manufacturers such as Intel or Samsung, which design and manufacture under one roof. Foundries serve multiple customers simultaneously, spreading their own massive capex across a broader customer base.

## Context and origin

The fabless model emerged in the early 1980s when manufacturing costs began rising beyond what most chip designers could justify alone. Companies like AMD and later Xilinx, Qualcomm, NVIDIA, and Broadcom moved away from captive manufacturing. The decisive validation came with the rise of TSMC in 1987, which proved that pure-play foundry manufacturing could match or exceed internal fab quality while enabling a new ecosystem of chip designers.

The model accelerated during the 1990s and 2000s as fab costs scaled past $5 billion for leading-edge facilities. Design investment became the differentiator while manufacturing became a separate, capital-intensive discipline. Today the fabless-foundry pairing is the backbone of most major chip categories except memory and certain analog segments.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

The fabless model creates three structural advantages. First, capital efficiency: fabless companies avoid the risk of owning a fab that may become obsolete, underutilized, or too expensive to upgrade. They can invest in product R&D and marketing instead. Second, flexibility: without captive manufacturing, fabless firms can tap multiple foundry nodes and shift production as process generations evolve. Third, focus: design teams can optimize purely for product performance without the constraints of internal manufacturing capabilities.

These advantages are why the model dominates in application processors, GPUs, FPGAs, networking chips, and most advanced logic. See [[Advanced Node Technology Roadmap]] for how node choice affects fabless product planning. [[Foundry Business Model]] explains the economic structure of the manufacturing partner.

The model also creates risks. Supply chain dependence is real: a foundry capacity crunch can delay product ramps as severely as a design failure. Pricing power with the foundry matters, and some fabless companies have stronger positions than others due to volume and strategic importance.

The relationship between fabless and foundry is not symmetric. TSMC's largest customers — Apple, NVIDIA, AMD — have significant leverage through volume, but even the largest fabless company does not control its manufacturing destiny the way an IDM does. This is a permanent structural feature of the model, not a temporary condition.

## Nuances critiques limits

A common critique is that fabless companies are "just" asset-light and therefore vulnerable if a foundry decides to prioritize another customer or raise prices. The foundry relationship is genuinely co-dependent, not purely transactional, because process optimization requires deep collaboration.

Another nuance is that not all fabless companies benefit equally from the model. Companies designing complex leading-edge chips benefit most because the foundry can absorb capex that would otherwise be prohibitive. Mature-node or specialty fabless companies may find the model less transformative because their manufacturing is less commoditized.

Finally, the model can create geographic concentration risk. Most leading-edge foundry capacity is in Taiwan, with Samsung and Intel Foundry Services as alternatives. This concentration has become a strategic concern for countries seeking semiconductor supply chain resilience.
