# Broadcom Business Analysis

> [!info] Summary
> Broadcom is a sprawling infrastructure semiconductor company with dominant positions in networking chips, custom ASICs for AI, wireless components, and enterprise storage. Its acquisition of VMware added software infrastructure to a hardware business already deeply embedded in the world's most critical networks, making it one of the most strategically important technology companies in AI and cloud infrastructure.

## Definition

Broadcom designs semiconductors and provides software for networking, broadband, wireless, storage, and enterprise infrastructure. Key semiconductor segments include: custom ASICs (AI accelerators for hyperscalers), Ethernet switches and PHY chips, wireless RF components, FC storage adapters, and GPS/wireless connectivity. The company is organized around infrastructure that connects, processes, and stores data at scale.

After acquiring VMware in 2023, Broadcom added substantial software revenue and margin, though integration complexity and customer reaction to VMware pricing changes have created controversy. The semiconductor business remains the core earnings driver, with AI custom ASICs as the fastest-growing sub-segment.

## Context and origin

Broadcom was founded in 1991 by Henry Samueli and Henry Nicholas III, grew rapidly in the networking and wireless chip markets, and accumulated a broad portfolio through aggressive acquisitions. The company was acquired by Avago Technologies in 2016 in one of the largest tech M&A deals, and the combined entity took the Broadcom name under CEO Hock Tan.

Under Hock Tan's leadership, Broadcom became known for operational discipline, margin expansion, and strategic acquisitions. The company resisted diversification beyond infrastructure semiconductors until the VMware deal, instead focusing on extracting value from a concentrated portfolio through customer concentration and pricing power.

## Mechanisms / characteristics / details

Broadcom's networking business is its foundational strength. Ethernet switches, PHYS, and network processors power the internal networks of hyperscale data centers, carrier infrastructure, and enterprise networks. The move to 100G/200G/400G Ethernet and the anticipated shift to 800G is a multi-year upgrade cycle that drives steady demand for new chips.

The AI custom ASIC business grew rapidly as hyperscalers sought alternatives or complements to NVIDIA GPUs. Broadcom's custom AI chips for Google (Titan) and other customers gave it a large and growing share of AI accelerator revenue without needing to build a general-purpose GPU ecosystem. See [[Custom Silicon ASIC Trend]] for the broader pattern.

The [[Cloud Data Center Infrastructure]] buildout benefits Broadcom across both networking and AI. Large AI clusters require high-bandwidth Ethernet networking between GPUs, switches, and storage, creating demand for both Broadcom's switch ASICs and its custom compute chips. This dual exposure is unusual and strategically valuable.

Wireless RF components remain important but lower-growth. Broadcom supplies filters, power amplifiers, and RF modules for smartphones, competing with Qorvo and Skyworks. The segment is tied to smartphone unit volumes and 5G adoption rather than AI dynamics.

The [[Foundry Business Model]] is central because Broadcom outsources all manufacturing to TSMC and Samsung. The company's scale gives it strong negotiating leverage on wafer pricing, but it also means supply constraints affect output. TSMC's capacity allocation for custom ASICs has been a bottleneck for Broadcom's AI chip growth.

## Nuances critiques limits

The VMware acquisition introduced significant software integration risk and customer controversy. Broadcom's aggressive pricing on VMware subscriptions has caused some customer backlash and driven interest in alternatives. The long-term software strategy and margin profile remains uncertain.

Customer concentration in custom ASICs is elevated. If major hyperscaler programs slow or shift to custom internal solutions, Broadcom's AI revenue could plateau. Unlike NVIDIA, Broadcom cannot easily replace lost custom ASIC customers with broad-based demand.

Geopolitical exposure through TSMC manufacturing and China-based customers also creates risk. Broadcom's revenue from Chinese customers has declined under export restrictions, affecting the wireless segment.
